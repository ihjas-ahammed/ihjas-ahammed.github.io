Semiconductor Basics: Definition, Properties, Bonds

Substances that have resistivity in between conductors and insulators are termed as semiconductors.

E.g.: Ge, Si, C etc. (Resistivity range ~ 10-4 to 104 Ωm)

Their difference is resistivity with resistance materials like nichrome (~10-6 Ωm) makes them prove they can't be used as resistance material (e.g. Nichrome 0.6 Ωm/m² ??).

Key Properties

Not only resistivity alone can decide a semiconductor, it has the following properties:

  1. Resistivity less than insulator but more than a conductor.
  2. Negative temperature coefficient (NTC): Resistance decreases with increase in temperature.
  3. By adding impurities (e.g., B, As, Cu ), we can alter its conduction properties.

Bonding and Structure

Semiconductors form covalent bonds by sharing four valence electrons.

These substances are arranged in a crystalline structure.

Ge, Si are mainly used semiconductors since their energy gap is very low. (e.g., 1.1 eV)

Germanium (Ge)

  • Energy Gap (ΔE) ≈ 0.7eV
  • 32 electrons
  • Expensive; it's a model substance
  • It can be purified relatively well and crystallized easily.

Silicon (Si)

  • Energy Gap (ΔE) ≈ 1.1 eV
  • 14 electrons
  • Most common semiconductor.

Energy Bands (Ge, Si)

Definition: In terms of energy bands, a semiconductor is a substance which has an almost filled valence band and nearly empty conduction band.

Temperature Effects on Semiconductors (SC)

i. At absolute zero (0 Kelvin)

  • No free electrons, since all of them are tightly held in covalent bonds.
  • So SC behaves as a perfect insulator.
  • Energy gap between valence and conduction band is too large for electrons to jump.

ii. Above absolute zero

  • A few free electrons exist, since some covalent bonds are broken due to thermal energy (heat).
  • These free electrons can cause current flow if potential is applied.
  • Resistance decreases as you heat up the SC (Negative Temperature Coefficient).
Note: Each time an electron moves to the conduction band as a free electron, a hole is created in the valence band.

Hole Current Concept

  • Thermal energy creates electron-hole pairs.
  • Hole movement is caused by holes attracting nearby valence electrons (filling the hole, creating a new one).
  • So it is actually due to the movement of valence electrons from one covalent bond to another.
  • Still, it seems like the positive holes are moving, hence it is called hole current.

Intrinsic vs Extrinsic SC; Doping

Intrinsic SC

An SC that is extremely pure form.

  • The current conduction is due to both holes and electrons generated thermally at room temperature, i.e., Inet = Ie + Ih.
  • In external wires, there are only electrons since they recombine with holes.

Extrinsic SC

Created by adding a small amount of impurity to an intrinsic SC to improve its conductivity.

  • The process of adding impurities is called doping.
  • It is done either to increase the number of free electrons (creating n-type) or holes (creating p-type).

N-type & P-type Semiconductors

N-type

  • Pentavalent impurity (5 valence electrons) is added to the intrinsic SC.
  • Examples: Arsenic (As) or Antimony (Sb). These are called donor impurities.
  • They provide more free electrons.
  • Current conduction is predominantly by free electrons (negative charge carriers), hence it is called n-type (electron type).
  • Makes the SC conduct more like ordinary metals (in terms of electron availability).

P-type

  • Trivalent impurity (3 valence electrons) is added to the intrinsic SC.
  • Examples: Gallium (Ga) or Indium (In). These are called acceptor impurities.
  • They provide more holes (vacancies for electrons).
  • Current conduction is predominantly because of holes, hence it is called p-type or hole-type conductivity.
  • Here, valence electrons move from one covalent bond to fill a hole in another, making the hole appear to move.
Note: Because of the fixed negative charge of the acceptor ions (which have accepted an electron to complete a bond), the net charge of the p-type material as a whole is zero (similarly for n-type with fixed positive donor ions).

Majority and Minority Carriers

For N-type:

  • Free electrons (from donor impurities) are majority carriers.
  • Holes (from thermal energy) are minority carriers.

For P-type:

  • Holes (from acceptor impurities) are majority carriers.
  • Free electrons (from thermal energy) are minority carriers.

PN Junction: Formation, Depletion and Barrier

The contact surface or interface formed when an n-type semiconductor is suitably joined to a p-type semiconductor is called a PN Junction.

It is the fundamental control element for many semiconductor devices (like diodes, transistors).

Formation

  • It is fabricated by special techniques.
  • E.g., Alloying:
    • A small piece of Indium (In - trivalent) is placed on a doped n-type Germanium (Ge) slab.
    • The system is heated to ~500°C.
    • Indium melts and fuses with the n-type Ge. Where they fuse, the In atoms create a p-type region, forming a PN junction.

Depletion Region

  • Due to the concentration difference, electrons from the n-region diffuse across the junction into the p-region, and holes from the p-region diffuse into the n-region.
  • Electrons combine with holes near the junction on the p-side, and holes combine with electrons near the junction on the n-side.
  • This leaves behind uncovered positive donor ions (+) on the n-side and uncovered negative acceptor ions (-) on the p-side, near the junction.
  • Creating a layer depleted of free charge carriers, containing only fixed ions. This is the depletion layer or depletion region.

The layer of fixed positive and negative ions creates an electric field across the junction, directed from the n-side to the p-side. This field opposes further diffusion.

This creates a potential difference across the junction called the barrier potential (V0) or contact potential.

  • For Germanium (Ge), V0 ≈ 0.3 V
  • For Silicon (Si), V0 ≈ 0.7 V

PN Junction Biasing (Forward/Reverse)

Bias: Using an external DC Voltage source to establish specific operating conditions for an electronic device like a PN junction diode.

Forward Biasing

  • Applying DC voltage in a direction that opposes the potential barrier.
  • Connect the positive (+ve) terminal of the battery to the p-type side and the negative (-ve) terminal to the n-type side.

[Image Placeholder: PN junction under forward bias (reduced depletion layer)]

  • The applied voltage reduces the barrier height and narrows the depletion layer.
  • Once the applied voltage overcomes the barrier potential (approx. 0.3V for Ge, 0.7V for Si), significant current starts to flow.
  • The PN junction offers a low resistance to current flow in forward bias (forward resistance).

Reverse Biasing

  • Applying DC voltage in such a way that it increases the potential barrier.
  • Connect the negative (-ve) terminal of the battery to the p-type side and the positive (+ve) terminal to the n-type side.

[Image Placeholder: PN junction under reverse bias (widened depletion layer)]

  • The applied voltage aids the barrier potential, increasing the barrier height and widening the depletion layer.
  • This opposes the flow of majority carriers across the junction.
  • The junction offers a very high resistance (reverse resistance).
  • Ideally, no current flows. In practice, a very small current (microamps or nanoamps) due to minority carriers flows (reverse saturation current).

Forward Bias Current Flow Mechanism

  • The free electrons (majority carriers) from the n-side are repelled by the negative terminal and pushed towards the junction.
  • The holes (majority carriers) from the p-side are repelled by the positive terminal and pushed towards the junction.
  • Electrons cross the junction, move through the p-side (by sequential movement through valence band/recombination), and reach the positive terminal.
  • Holes cross the junction, move through the n-side (effectively), and are neutralized by electrons from the negative terminal near the contact.
  • Electrons are continuously supplied by the negative terminal to the n-side, and removed from the p-side by the positive terminal, maintaining the current flow.
